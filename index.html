Coos Bay is the commercial and cultural heart of the Southern Oregon Coast. With a deep-water port, thriving arts district, and direct access to miles of beaches and the Oregon Dunes, Coos Bay offers a full-service coastal city lifestyle at a fraction of the cost of Portland or Bend.

The housing market in Coos Bay is among the most dynamic on the coast, with a median price of $349K and a remarkable 22.9% year-over-year appreciation as of February 2026. Homes are moving quickly — averaging just 17 days on market — making it a seller's market that still offers tremendous long-term value for buyers.

Neighborhoods range from historic Victorian homes in Marshfield to newer developments near Empire Lakes and waterfront properties along the bay. The city's growing restaurant scene, galleries, and annual events like the Blackberry Arts Festival make it one of Oregon's most underrated coastal destinations.

Insider Knowledge

Tips for Buying on the Oregon Coast

Coastal real estate has unique considerations that many buyers — especially those coming from inland markets — aren't aware of. Here's what you need to know.

🌊

Understand Flood Zones

Many coastal properties fall within FEMA flood zones. This affects your insurance requirements and premiums significantly. Always verify the flood zone designation before making an offer, and factor flood insurance costs into your monthly budget. I'll help you interpret FEMA maps and understand what each zone designation means for your property.

🏠

Budget for Coastal Maintenance

The salt air, wind, rain, and humidity of the Oregon Coast accelerate wear on roofs, siding, decks, and windows. Budget for higher maintenance costs than you'd expect in an inland climate — typically 1.5–2% of the home's value per year. Regular painting, deck sealing, and roof inspections are essential to protecting your investment.

💧

Well & Septic Systems

Many rural coastal properties use private wells and septic systems rather than city utilities. Always test the well water quality and have the septic system professionally inspected before closing. These systems can be expensive to repair or replace — knowing their condition upfront prevents costly surprises after you move in.

📋

Oregon Coastal Zone Management

Oregon has strong shoreland protections through the Oregon Coastal Management Program. Some oceanfront and bay-adjacent properties have restrictions on development, additions, or certain uses. If you're buying waterfront or planning to modify a coastal property, we'll review applicable land-use regulations early in the process.

📊

Act Quickly in Hot Markets

Coos Bay properties are averaging just 17 days on market in early 2026. In a competitive market, being prepared is everything — pre-approval in hand, clear criteria defined, and a trusted agent ready to submit an offer. Don't let the right home slip through while you're still getting organized.

🏦

Explore Oregon-Specific Programs

Oregon offers several programs for first-time homebuyers including Oregon Bond Residential Loan Program with below-market interest rates and down payment assistance. The Oregon Individual Development Account Initiative can also help qualified buyers save toward a down payment. I'll point you toward the resources you qualify for.

Frequently Asked Questions

Honest answers to the questions buyers and sellers ask most often about the Southern Oregon Coast real estate market.

FAQ List

What makes the Southern Oregon Coast a great place to buy property?

The Southern Oregon Coast offers a rare combination of natural beauty, affordability, and livability that's increasingly hard to find on the West Coast. Coos Bay, North Bend, Bandon, and Coquille each offer their own distinct character — from the vibrant harbor culture of Coos Bay to the world-class golf and dramatic sea stacks of Bandon. The region enjoys mild, year-round temperatures moderated by the Pacific, stunning beaches that rarely feel crowded, and access to incredible outdoor recreation including hiking, fishing, crabbing, kayaking, and wildlife watching. Compared to California or even Portland, property values remain remarkably reasonable, meaning your real estate dollar goes much further here. And as remote work has given buyers more geographic freedom, the Southern Oregon Coast is attracting a new wave of buyers who want quality of life without big-city prices.

How is the current real estate market in Coos Bay?

As of February 2026, the Coos Bay market is among the most active on the Oregon Coast. The median sale price is $349,000 — representing a significant 22.9% year-over-year increase — and homes are averaging just 17 days on market, which is exceptionally fast for a coastal market. There are currently 262 active listings. These conditions indicate a seller-favored market where well-priced, well-presented homes are generating strong buyer interest and, in some cases, multiple offers. Buyers who come prepared with financing in order can still find excellent value compared to larger Oregon metro areas, but they should be ready to move decisively when the right property appears. Market conditions can shift, so I always recommend getting current data before making any buying or selling decision.

What types of properties are available in the Coos Bay area?

The Southern Oregon Coast offers a genuinely diverse range of property types to suit virtually any buyer profile. In Coos Bay and North Bend, you'll find historic Craftsman and Victorian homes, mid-century ranches, waterfront properties along the bay, newer construction developments, and small-scale multi-family investments. Bandon skews toward higher-end single-family homes, oceanview estates, and vacation-oriented properties. Coquille and the surrounding Coos County rural areas offer farmhouses, acreage properties, ranches, and homes with outbuildings — often at prices well below coastal city levels. Across the region, there are also condominiums, manufactured homes on land, and small commercial-residential mixed-use properties. If you have a specific property type in mind, let's talk — I can tell you what's currently available and what to realistically expect in your budget range.

What should I know about buying coastal property in Oregon?

Oregon's coastline is governed by a unique set of laws and regulations that differ meaningfully from other states. The Oregon Beach Bill of 1967 established that all Oregon ocean beaches are public property — which is wonderful for recreation but creates specific easement and access considerations for oceanfront property owners. The Oregon Coastal Management Program governs development within the coastal zone, and properties near the shoreline may have setback requirements, height limitations, or restrictions on certain types of development. Flood zone designations affect insurance requirements and costs significantly — some coastal properties are required to carry flood insurance as a condition of their mortgage. Salt air and coastal weather accelerate wear on structures, so inspection thoroughness matters more than it does inland. I help every coastal buyer navigate these considerations before they sign anything.

How do property taxes work in Coos County?

Coos County property taxes in Oregon are governed by the state's unique Measure 5 and Measure 50 framework, passed by Oregon voters in the 1990s. Under this system, property tax bills are tied to "assessed value" — which is typically lower than the property's real market value — and are limited to a 3% annual increase regardless of how much market values rise. This means you may buy a home at $349K market value but be taxed on a significantly lower assessed value if the previous owner held the property long-term. The effective tax rate in Coos County typically runs around 1.0–1.2% of assessed value, depending on local levies. When you sell, the assessed value resets to current market value, which can increase taxes for the new buyer. I always recommend consulting with a tax professional to fully understand your specific property's tax situation before purchase.

What is the average time to close on a property in this area?

In the Southern Oregon Coast area, a standard residential real estate transaction typically takes 30–45 days from accepted offer to closing when using conventional or FHA financing. Cash transactions can close in as few as 10–14 days if all parties are motivated. VA loans tend to take slightly longer — often 45–60 days — due to the additional appraisal requirements. Rural properties with well and septic systems may add time for additional inspections, lab results, and any repairs required. If the appraisal comes in below the agreed purchase price, negotiations over price or repairs can extend the timeline. I set realistic expectations with every client at the outset and work proactively with lenders, inspectors, and title officers to keep every transaction moving on schedule.

Are there any special inspections needed for coastal properties?

Yes — coastal properties warrant a more comprehensive inspection approach than typical inland homes. Beyond the standard general home inspection, I typically recommend that coastal buyers consider: a roof and gutter inspection with attention to moss and moisture damage common in the wet Oregon climate; a structural inspection focused on the foundation, crawl space moisture levels, and any signs of wood rot or pest damage; a well and septic inspection for rural properties; radon testing; and an oil tank search if the property is older and may have had an underground heating oil tank. For properties in or near flood zones, I also recommend reviewing elevation certificates and FEMA flood maps. For oceanfront properties, a coastal engineer can assess bluff stability and erosion risk. These inspections add modest cost upfront but can save you from expensive surprises after closing.

What financing options are available for coastal properties?

Most standard financing options are available for Southern Oregon Coast properties, including conventional 30- and 15-year fixed mortgages, FHA loans, and VA loans for eligible veterans. USDA Rural Development loans are also available in many parts of Coos County and can provide 100% financing for qualified buyers in designated rural areas — a significant benefit for buyers targeting Coquille and surrounding communities. For investment properties and vacation homes, lenders typically require larger down payments (often 20–25%) and charge slightly higher rates than for primary residences. Jumbo loans are available for higher-priced Bandon properties. One consideration unique to coastal areas: some lenders add requirements for flood insurance to be in force at closing for flood zone properties. I work with a network of Oregon-based lenders who understand these nuances and can help you find the most competitive terms for your specific situation.

How does the cost of living compare to other Oregon areas?

The Southern Oregon Coast offers a genuinely lower cost of living compared to Oregon's major metro areas. Housing is the most dramatic difference — a dollar goes significantly further in Coos Bay or Coquille than in Portland, Bend, or Eugene. Groceries and everyday goods are comparable to statewide averages, though the distance from major distribution centers means some specialty items can cost slightly more. Healthcare is available locally through Bay Area Hospital and associated clinics, though residents sometimes travel to Eugene or Medford for specialized care. Utilities are reasonable — the mild ocean climate reduces extreme heating and cooling costs compared to inland Oregon. Oregon has no sales tax, which is a meaningful benefit for residents. Overall, many buyers relocating from California or out-of-state metros find that their money stretches considerably further on the Southern Oregon Coast without sacrificing quality of life.

What are the best neighborhoods in Coos Bay for families?

Coos Bay has several excellent areas that are particularly popular with families. The Empire District offers a quieter, residential feel with larger lots, proximity to Empire Lakes, and a strong sense of neighborhood community. Sunset Heights provides elevated views, newer construction, and a suburban feel with easy access to schools and shopping. The Marshfield area features beautiful historic homes and walkability to downtown amenities. For families specifically, proximity to good schools is often the priority — Coos Bay School District serves the area and includes Marshfield High School, which has strong academic and athletic programs. I always recommend visiting neighborhoods at different times of day and on weekends to get a genuine feel for the pace and character of each area before making a commitment.

What should I know about flood zones and insurance on the coast?

Flood zone awareness is one of the most important considerations in Southern Oregon Coast real estate. FEMA maintains National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP) maps that designate areas by flood risk. Properties in high-risk zones (Zone A or AE) are typically required by lenders to carry flood insurance as a condition of the mortgage. Flood insurance is purchased separately from homeowner's insurance and is priced based on the property's elevation relative to the base flood elevation, the type of structure, and coverage levels. For some lower-lying bay-adjacent properties, flood insurance premiums can be substantial — sometimes $200–$500 or more per month — and must be factored into your total housing costs. I always verify flood zone status for every property we consider and can help you request an elevation certificate, which sometimes reveals that a property is actually at lower risk than its zone designation suggests, resulting in significant insurance savings.

How competitive is the current market for buyers?

Market competitiveness varies significantly by city and price point. Coos Bay is the most competitive market currently, with homes selling in an average of 17 days and strong year-over-year price growth of 22.9% — indicating a seller's market where prepared buyers with financing ready can succeed but need to move quickly. North Bend is similarly active at 20 days on market. Bandon and Coquille offer more breathing room, with longer days on market giving buyers more time to deliberate. Across the region, well-priced homes in desirable conditions still attract strong interest. My advice for buyers in competitive markets: get pre-approved before you start touring, know exactly what your non-negotiables are, and be prepared to write a strong offer on the day you see the right property. I'll guide you on offer strategy, appropriate escalation clauses, and which contingencies are standard vs. those you might consider waiving in competitive situations.

What are the property management options if I'm buying a vacation home?

The Southern Oregon Coast has a healthy vacation rental market, particularly in Bandon with its proximity to Bandon Dunes Golf Resort, which drives strong demand from golf tourists year-round. Several professional property management companies operate in the area and can handle everything from listing on Airbnb and VRBO to cleaning, guest communication, key management, and maintenance coordination. Management fees typically run 20–35% of rental revenue depending on the level of service. Before purchasing a vacation rental, verify the local short-term rental regulations — Coos County and individual cities have varying rules about licensing, occupancy limits, and permitted zones. I can connect you with local property managers who will give you honest projections based on comparable properties, helping you model realistic income expectations before you purchase.

What is the rental market like in the Coos Bay area?

The long-term rental market in the Coos Bay area is tight, with rental vacancy rates historically low and demand consistently outpacing available supply. This creates an attractive environment for investors — single-family rental homes in Coos Bay and North Bend can generate reliable rental income with relatively low vacancy periods. Average monthly rents for a 3-bedroom home in Coos Bay range approximately from $1,400 to $1,900 depending on condition, location, and features, though rents have trended upward along with sale prices. For investors, the math on rental yields in this market can be more favorable than in overheated coastal markets in California or Washington. I work with a number of investor clients and can provide detailed rental market analysis, help you identify properties with strong rental potential, and connect you with experienced local property managers to handle day-to-day operations.

What are the key differences between the six main cities?

Each of the six communities has a distinct personality that suits different buyer profiles. Coos Bay is the largest, most urban option — a real working city with the full range of services, employers, healthcare, shopping, and cultural amenities, plus a dynamic harbor and arts scene. North Bend is Coos Bay's quieter twin across the bay, offering slightly lower prices, access to the Oregon Dunes, and a more residential, family-oriented pace. Bandon is a premium coastal destination — smaller, quieter, and more expensive, with world-class golf at Bandon Dunes, dramatic oceanfront scenery, and an artsy Old Town that draws visitors year-round. Coquille is the inland option — the most affordable, the most rural, and the most small-town in character, ideal for buyers who want space, privacy, and acreage over beach proximity. Florence offers a buyer-friendly market at the gateway to the Oregon Dunes National Recreation Area, with a charming Old Town along the Siuslaw River, Sea Lion Caves nearby, and a wide selection of river and ocean-view properties — a great fit for buyers seeking value with world-class natural beauty. Brookings, Oregon's "Banana Belt" at the southern tip of the coast, boasts the mildest climate in Oregon, dramatic scenery along the Samuel Boardman Scenic Corridor, and a market that is appreciating rapidly — making it an increasingly sought-after destination for retirees, outdoor enthusiasts, and savvy investors. My job is to listen to who you are and what you want from this place, then help you find the community that genuinely fits your life.

Is now a good time to sell my property on the Oregon Coast?

Market timing varies by community, but in Coos Bay, the current conditions are notably favorable for sellers. With a 22.9% year-over-year price increase and homes selling in an average of 17 days, well-priced Coos Bay properties are generating strong buyer interest. North Bend is similarly active at 20 days on market. In Bandon and Coquille, where days on market are longer, a strategic approach to pricing and presentation matters more — but qualified buyers are still active in these markets. The best time to sell is when it aligns with your personal circumstances and financial goals, regardless of market conditions. I'll give you an honest assessment of what your specific property is worth today, what competing inventory looks like, and what realistic expectations are for your market segment — so you can make an informed decision rather than guessing at timing.

What improvements offer the best ROI for coastal homes?

For coastal Oregon homes, the improvements that consistently deliver the best return on investment focus on condition, curb appeal, and coastal durability. A well-maintained roof is the single biggest factor — buyers and their inspectors scrutinize roofs closely in the rainy Oregon climate, and a roof with remaining life commands higher offers and faster sales. Fresh exterior paint (specifically products rated for coastal salt-air environments) dramatically improves first impressions. Updating kitchens and bathrooms — even with modest refreshes like new fixtures, cabinet hardware, and countertops — consistently tests well with buyers. Improving energy efficiency through new windows or insulation is increasingly valued. For properties with ocean or bay views, anything that improves the view experience (window cleaning, deck refinishing, vegetation management) can pay dividends. Avoid over-improving for the neighborhood — I'll help you identify the improvements with the best realistic return for your specific property and price point.

How long does it typically take to sell a home in the area?

Time on market varies considerably by city, price point, and condition. In Coos Bay, homes are averaging 17 days on market as of February 2026 — meaning well-priced, well-presented homes are often selling within two to three weeks of listing. North Bend averages 20 days. Coquille homes average 51 days, and Bandon homes average 146 days — which reflects the more deliberate pace of higher-priced and vacation-oriented buying decisions in that market. Properties that are priced appropriately for current conditions, presented with professional photography, and marketed broadly will always sell faster than those that are overpriced or poorly marketed. Conversely, a home that sits on the market for months without offers is sending a signal to buyers — usually about price. I'll be straightforward with you about pricing strategy from the start, so you avoid that scenario entirely.
